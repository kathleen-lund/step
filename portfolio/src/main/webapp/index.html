Hi, my name is Katie, and I am a rising junior at Washington University in St. Louis. I'm studying computer science and cognitive neuroscience. I am originally from a suburb of Chicago called Libertyville, IL. For fun, I like to play my cello or have family game nights!

At school, I play cello in the symphony orchestra. I love to try new brunch places with my friends and get mozzarella sticks on Fridays at the cafeteria. I am also the president of Women in Computer Science on campus. I love being active in the engineering community at WashU!

Some projects I've worked on...
Project COVID
Worked as a Senior Mobile App Developer helping to develop a React Native app to distribute unbiased information about COVID-19. Contributed mainly to the frontend. The app is currently in review on the iOS App Store.
Invisibly
Interned last summer for Square co-founder Jim McKelvey's startup. Worked on creative company side-projects, such as a "tinder for movie trailers" Android mobile app and a driving game app to gamify earning money from ads. Used Kotlin, XML, Python, and C# in Unity.
Hengenlab
Began working as a student research assistant in the Hengenlab at WashU. This is a computational neuroscience lab. In-person lab work included building electrodes for implantation in mice, assisting with surgeries, and discussing analysis with my MD/PhD mentor. Remote work included analyzing neural data using Python/JupyterLab.

Blog Posts!

Hidden Talent 1: Bucket Ball

I have a secret love for bucket balls. Not only am I randomly good at tossing balls in incrementally spaced buckets, but I've had lots of opportunity to do so. Some of my fondest memories are tossing softballs back into the ball bucket from many yards away at the end of practice, to the delight of my teammates.

Hidden Talent 2: Double Jointedness

My fingers are very double-jointed. I can bend them all downward at the first joint behind the nail, and my thumbs bend backwards in a hitchhiker's thumb position. This double-jointedness actually makes it a bit difficult to play the cello, as collapsing joints are a big no-no for your left hand.
